"Competition is a waste of money, taking something someone else is doing and trying to copy them wasting R&D dollars instead of using them to differentiate."

Jonathan Ross, Groq CEO, November 5th 2025, explaining why Groq wasn't competing with Nvidia.

49 days later, Nvidia paid $20 billion for Groq's assets and hired Ross along with his entire executive team.

But here's the thing: this wasn't actually an acquisition. At least not in the traditional sense. Nvidia paid $20 billion for Groq's IP and people, but explicitly did NOT buy the company. Jensen Huang's statement was surgical: "While we are adding talented employees to our ranks and licensing Groq's IP, we are not acquiring Groq as a company."

That phrasing is the entire story. Because what Nvidia carved out of the deal tells you everything about why this happened.

## What Nvidia Actually Bought (And What It Didn't)

What Nvidia acquired:
- All of Groq's intellectual property and patents
- Non-exclusive licensing rights to Groq's inference technology
- Jonathan Ross (CEO), Sunny Madra (President), and the entire senior leadership team

What Nvidia explicitly did NOT buy:
- GroqCloud (the cloud infrastructure business)

GroqCloud continues as an independent company under CFO Simon Edwards. This is Nvidia's largest acquisition ever (previous record was Mellanox at $7B in 2019), and they structured it to leave the actual operating business behind. That doesn't happen by accident.

The reason is geopolitical, and it stems from a deal Groq signed seven months earlier.

## The Valuation Math Doesn't Add Up (Unless You Understand What Nvidia Really Bought)

Groq raised $750 million in September 2025 at a post-money valuation of $6.9 billion.

Yesterday, three months later, Nvidia paid $20 billion. That's a $13.1 billion premium over the last valuation, or roughly 3x what investors thought the company was worth 90 days earlier.

For context, that $20B represents approximately 40x Groq's target revenue. Compare that to Anthropic's recent funding round, which valued the company at roughly 20x revenue. Nvidia paid double the multiple for a company with slashed revenue projections (The Information reported in July 2025 that Groq had cut over $1 billion from its 2025 revenue forecast, pushing those expectations to 2026).

Here's the uncomfortable question: why would Nvidia pay $13.1 billion more than the market rate for technology they could build? Nvidia has the PDK, the volume, the talent, and the infrastructure. They could ship a competitive SRAM-based inference architecture before Groq could take meaningful market share.

The answer is that the technology was worth $6.9B (what the market said in September). The other $13.1B was paying for solutions to multiple strategic problems:

1. **Geopolitical cleanup**: The Saudi deal was a regulatory time bomb. CFIUS scrutiny, congressional questions, export control complications. Making that disappear? Worth billions.

2. **Neutralizing Meta/Llama**: The April 2025 partnership gave Groq distribution to millions of developers. If Llama + Groq became the default open-source inference stack, that's a structural threat to Nvidia's ecosystem.

3. **Eliminating GroqCloud**: Nvidia has invested in other cloud AI providers. GroqCloud was a competitor. The carve-out ensures it dies.

4. **Political access**: Chamath (the bastard) makes $2B when you consider that Social Capital's stake was probably ~10% after dilution. David Sacks (another bastard) looks good (major AI deal under his watch as AI Czar). Nvidia gets favourable regulatory treatment.

5. **Regulatory arbitrage**: Non-exclusive licensing avoids antitrust scrutiny. Traditional acquisition of this size? Years of regulatory review. Non-exclusive licensing? Clean. No monopoly concerns because "anyone can license it." But in practice, Nvidia gets the talent (Ross + team can't work for competitors), the IP (can integrate before anyone else), and the elimination of GroqCloud.

That $13.1B premium is the price of solving all these problems simultaneously, announced on Christmas Eve when media and YouTubers are inactive. By the time everyone's back from vacation, it's old news and the Chamath/Sacks/Trump connections get less scrutiny.

## The Saudi Arabia Problem

In February 2025, Saudi Arabia committed $1.5 billion to expand Groq's Dammam data center. The publicly stated goal was supporting SDAIA's ALLaM, Saudi Arabia's Arabic large language model. The actual goal was Vision 2030: positioning the Kingdom as an AI superpower.

Groq built the region's largest inference cluster in eight days in December 2024. From that Dammam facility, GroqCloud serves "nearly four billion people regionally adjacent to the KSA." This isn't API access. This is critical AI infrastructure for a nation-state, funded by the Public Investment Fund, processing inference workloads at national scale.

According to Ross in the Series E announcement, Groq powers Humain's services including the Humain chat product and supported OpenAI's GPT-OSS model release in Saudi Arabia. Groq operates 13 facilities across the US, Canada, Europe, and the Middle East. Ross noted that capacity expanded more than 10% in the month before the funding announcement and all of that capacity was already in use. Customers were asking for more capacity than Groq could satisfy.

That creates a CFIUS (Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States) problem. A U.S. chip company, venture-backed by American investors, building sovereign AI capability for Saudi Arabia. If Nvidia had acquired GroqCloud outright, they would inherit those contracts and the regulatory scrutiny that comes with them. Foreign investment reviews, export control questions, congressional inquiries about why an American company is providing cutting-edge AI to a Middle Eastern monarchy.

By carving out GroqCloud, Nvidia gets the technology and the talent without the geopolitical mess. The Saudi contracts stay with Edwards and the independent GroqCloud entity. Clean separation. No CFIUS entanglement.

But let's be clear about what this carve-out actually means: **GroqCloud is meant to die.** The question "how does GroqCloud operate without the hardware IP?" answers itself. It doesn't. Without Ross and the engineering team, without the IP rights, without Nvidia's support, GroqCloud will quietly wind down over the next 12-18 months. The Saudi contracts will get restructured or redirected. The Dammam facility either scales down or gets sold to a non-U.S. entity. This wasn't a carve-out to preserve GroqCloud. It was a carve-out to avoid inheriting the Saudi problem while ensuring GroqCloud dies anyway.

And Nvidia's cloud partners (the ones Nvidia has invested in) are happy. One less competitor.

## LPU vs TPU vs CPU: Why SRAM Matters

To understand why Nvidia paid anything for Groq, you need to understand the architectural bet Ross made when he left Google.

**CPUs and GPUs** are built around external DRAM/HBM (High Bandwidth Memory). Every compute operation requires shuttling data between the processor and off-chip memory. This works fine for general-purpose computing, but for inference workloads, that constant round-trip creates latency and energy overhead. GPUs evolved from graphics rendering, so they're optimized for parallel training workloads, not sequential inference.

**TPUs (Google's Tensor Processing Units)** reduce some of this overhead with larger on-chip buffers and a systolic array architecture, but they still rely on HBM for model weights and activations. They're deterministic in execution but non-deterministic in memory access patterns.

**LPUs (Groq's Language Processing Units)** take a different approach: massive on-chip SRAM instead of external DRAM/HBM. The entire model (for models that fit) lives in SRAM with 80 TB/s of bandwidth and 230 MB capacity per chip. No off-chip memory bottleneck. No dynamic scheduling. The architecture is entirely deterministic from compilation to execution. You know exactly what happens at each cycle on each chip at each moment.

This creates massive advantages for inference:
- Llama 2 7B: 750 tokens/sec (2048 token context)
- Llama 2 70B: 300 tokens/sec (4096 token context)
- Mixtral 8x7B: 480 tokens/sec (4096 token context)

And 10x better energy efficiency because you're not constantly moving data across a memory bus.

The trade-off is brutal: only 14GB of SRAM per rack means you can't run Llama 3.1 405B. And LPUs can't train models at all. This is an inference-only architecture with limited model size support.

But here's what makes this interesting: if DRAM/HBM prices continue climbing (which they are), and if inference becomes the dominant AI workload (which it is), SRAM-based architectures become economically compelling despite the size limitations. Most production AI applications aren't running 405B models. They're running 7B-70B models that need low latency and high throughput.

One theory circulating is that OpenAI's DRAM land grab is targeting non-Nvidia platforms, which would make Nvidia's GPU-based inference less competitive on cost. An SRAM-based architecture hedges against that. But even if that's true, Nvidia could develop a competitive SRAM design in one development cycle. They have everything they need.

So why pay $20B for it? Because they need it NOW, and the $13.1B premium is buying more than just technical capability.

## The All-In Connection

This gets messier when you look at who was involved. Chamath Palihapitiya, through Social Capital, led Groq's initial $10 million investment in 2017 at a $25 million pre-money valuation. Social Capital secured 28.57% of the company and a board seat for Chamath.

David Sacks, Chamath's co-host on the All-In podcast, became Trump's AI and Crypto Czar in late 2024. In July 2025, Sacks co-authored "America's AI Action Plan," a White House strategy document positioning AI as a matter of national security. The plan called for exporting "the full AI technology stack to all countries willing to join America's AI alliance" while preventing adversarial nations from building independent AI capabilities.

Two months later at the All-In Summit in September 2025, Tareq Amin (CEO of HUMAIN, Saudi Arabia's state-backed AI company) presented Groq as "the American AI stack in action." This was seven months after the $1.5B Saudi deal.

Sunny Madra, Groq's President and COO, was actively promoting the All-In narrative during this period. He appeared on the All-In podcast in March 2024 to provide a "Groq update" and joined Sacks on "This Week in Startups" in November 2023. When Anthropic raised AI safety regulation concerns in October 2025, Madra publicly sided with Sacks, suggesting "one company is causing chaos for the entire industry" and echoing Sacks's accusation that Anthropic was engaged in "regulatory capture."

So you have Sacks pushing an "America First" AI policy from the White House while Chamath's portfolio company (where Madra is President) is building AI infrastructure for Saudi Arabia. Then Groq gets presented at the All-In Summit as an example of American AI leadership. Three months later, announced on Christmas Eve when media coverage is minimal, Nvidia pays $20 billion to clean up the geopolitical contradiction.

Chamath walks away with $1.6B to $2.4B. Sacks gets a major AI deal under his watch. Nvidia gets favorable regulatory treatment and eliminates multiple problems. The timing ensures minimal scrutiny of these connections.

The Groq bet was classic venture capital: concentrated bet on an exceptional founder (Jonathan Ross, the engineer who invented Google's TPU) building non-obvious technology. But retail investors never got access to deals like Groq. They got Virgin Galactic.

## What This Actually Means

Nvidia just made its largest acquisition ever to acquire inference technology from a nine-year-old startup valued at $6.9B three months earlier, paying a $13.1B premium to avoid inheriting geopolitical liabilities while eliminating competitive threats. That tells you several things:

**The technology was worth what the market said it was worth**: $6.9B. Sophisticated investors including BlackRock and Neuberger Berman underwrote that valuation 90 days before the Nvidia deal. The $13.1B premium is what Nvidia paid for everything else.

**Inference is the battleground, but competition isn't coming from Groq**: Nvidia's real threats are Google's TPU, Amazon's Trainium, AMD's Instinct, and Intel's ARC. Groq wasn't going to take meaningful market share from any of them. But Groq created other problems (geopolitical, ecosystem) that justified the premium to eliminate.

**Geopolitics constrains deals in ways traditional antitrust doesn't capture**: The GroqCloud carve-out is precedent-setting. When startups build infrastructure for nation-states with complicated U.S. relations, it creates acquisition complications that traditional M&A can't solve. Non-exclusive licensing is regulatory arbitrage that lets dominant players neutralize threats without traditional acquisition scrutiny.

**The "America First" AI narrative is transactional**: When the people promoting American AI dominance are simultaneously cutting deals with Saudi Arabia, and those contradictions require $20 billion to resolve while enriching political allies, it raises questions about whose interests the "American AI Alliance" actually serves.

**Access to exceptional founders is gatekept, and the returns are privatized**: Chamath got access to Jonathan Ross in 2017 because of Social Capital's network and reputation. That single relationship generated $2 billion. Retail investors got SPACs that averaged 14% losses. The system is designed this way.

**Timing matters**: Announcing on Christmas Eve when media and YouTubers are inactive ensures the Chamath/Sacks/Trump connections get minimal scrutiny. By the time everyone's back from vacation, it's old news.

Ross said in November that Groq wasn't competing with Nvidia because "competition is a waste of money." He was right, just not in the way he meant. You can't compete with someone who has $20 billion to acquire you and the political connections to make the deal happen over Christmas Eve. Especially when your investors' geopolitical deal-making creates liabilities that only an acquisition can resolve.

Nvidia paid $6.9B for technology and $13.1B to solve everything else. That's how business works when one company has a dominant position and a massive war chest. It's literally the thesis Peter Thiel sets out in Zero to One: once you have dominance, you just buy any business that has potential to disrupt your revenue. And when regulation hasn't caught up to non-exclusive licensing deals, you can do it without traditional antitrust scrutiny.
