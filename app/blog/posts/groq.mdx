date: "2025-12-25"
description: "On November 5th, Groq's CEO said they weren't competing with Nvidia. On December 24th, Nvidia paid $20 billion for Groq's IP and team. What the hell happened?"
---

"Competition is a waste of money, taking something someone else is doing and trying to copy them wasting R&D dollars instead of using them to differentiate."

Jonathan Ross, Groq CEO, November 5th 2025, explaining why Groq wasn't competing with Nvidia.

49 days later, Nvidia paid $20 billion for Groq's assets and hired Ross along with his entire executive team.

But here's the thing: this wasn't actually an acquisition. At least not in the traditional sense. Nvidia paid $20 billion for Groq's IP and people, but explicitly did NOT buy the company. Jensen Huang's statement was surgical: "While we are adding talented employees to our ranks and licensing Groq's IP, we are not acquiring Groq as a company."

That phrasing is the entire story. Because what Nvidia carved out of the deal tells you everything about why this happened.

## What Nvidia Actually Bought (And What It Didn't)

What Nvidia acquired:
- All of Groq's intellectual property and patents
- Non-exclusive licensing rights to Groq's inference technology
- Jonathan Ross (CEO), Sunny Madra (President), and the entire senior leadership team

What Nvidia explicitly did NOT buy:
- GroqCloud (the cloud infrastructure business)

GroqCloud continues as an independent company under CFO Simon Edwards. This is Nvidia's largest acquisition ever (previous record was Mellanox at $7B in 2019), and they structured it to leave the actual operating business behind. That doesn't happen by accident.

The reason is geopolitical, and it stems from a deal Groq signed seven months earlier.

## The Saudi Arabia Problem

In February 2025, Saudi Arabia committed $1.5 billion to expand Groq's Dammam data center. The publicly stated goal was supporting SDAIA's ALLaM, Saudi Arabia's Arabic large language model. The actual goal was Vision 2030: positioning the Kingdom as an AI superpower.

Groq built the region's largest inference cluster in eight days in December 2024. From that Dammam facility, GroqCloud now serves "nearly four billion people regionally adjacent to the KSA." This isn't just selling API access to Saudi developers. This is critical AI infrastructure for a nation-state, funded by the Public Investment Fund, processing inference workloads at national scale.

That creates a CFIUS (Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States) problem. A U.S. chip company, venture-backed by American investors, building sovereign AI capability for Saudi Arabia. If Nvidia had acquired GroqCloud outright, they would inherit those contracts and the regulatory scrutiny that comes with them. Foreign investment reviews, export control questions, congressional inquiries about why an American company is providing cutting-edge AI to a Middle Eastern monarchy.

By carving out GroqCloud, Nvidia gets the technology and the talent without the geopolitical mess. The Saudi contracts stay with Edwards and the independent GroqCloud entity. Clean separation. No CFIUS entanglement.

My guess is GroqCloud quietly winds down over the next 12-18 months as Nvidia integrates Groq's technology into its own stack, but that gives Saudi Arabia time to save face and transition to alternative infrastructure. The $1.5 billion commitment probably gets restructured or redirected. The Dammam facility either scales down or gets sold to a non-U.S. entity.

This deal structure wasn't about simplifying the acquisition. It was about extracting what Nvidia wanted while leaving the geopolitical liability to operate independently.

## Why Nvidia Paid $20 Billion for Inference IP

Jonathan Ross didn't leave Google to build a better GPU. He designed something fundamentally different: the LPU (Language Processing Unit), a deterministic architecture where the system knows exactly what happens at each stage on each chip at each moment from the moment a workload is compiled. No dynamic scheduling, no cache misses, no router overhead.

GPUs evolved from graphics rendering and constantly shuttle data between off-chip high-bandwidth memory because they're non-deterministic by design. That round-trip overhead kills performance for certain inference workloads.

The performance difference in production:
- Llama 2 7B: 750 tokens/sec (2048 token context)
- Llama 2 70B: 300 tokens/sec (4096 token context)
- Mixtral 8x7B: 480 tokens/sec (4096 token context)

Groq's LPU is also 10x more energy efficient than GPUs for inference. The trade-off is limited memory (14GB per rack) and no training capability, making it unsuitable for the largest models. But for real-time, low-latency inference at scale, Groq was genuinely better than Nvidia's hardware on specific workloads.

That created two problems for Nvidia:

**Commercial threat**: In April 2025, Meta officially partnered with Groq to power the Llama API. Meta open-sourced Llama specifically to commoditize the inference layer and create alternatives to closed ecosystems. If Groq became the default inference infrastructure for the Llama ecosystem, Nvidia's dominance in AI infrastructure would erode. Ross stated that "teaming up with Meta for the official Llama API raises the bar for model performance." That partnership gave Groq distribution to millions of developers.

**Geopolitical leverage**: The Saudi deal made Groq untouchable through normal competitive means. You can't undercut a competitor building state-backed infrastructure with sovereign wealth fund capital. And if Groq continued scaling internationally with nation-state backing, they'd become a true alternative to Nvidia's stack with geopolitical protection.

Nvidia was facing a scenario where a venture-backed startup with superior inference technology was building both a commercial competitor (via Meta/Llama) and a geopolitically protected infrastructure business (via Saudi Arabia). The only clean solution was acquisition, structured carefully to avoid inheriting the Saudi entanglement.

## The All-In Connection

This gets messier when you look at who was involved. Chamath Palihapitiya, through Social Capital, led Groq's initial $10 million investment in 2017 at a $25 million pre-money valuation. Social Capital secured 28.57% of the company and a board seat for Chamath.

David Sacks, Chamath's co-host on the All-In podcast, became Trump's AI and Crypto Czar in late 2024. In July 2025, Sacks co-authored "America's AI Action Plan," a White House strategy document positioning AI as a matter of national security. The plan called for exporting "the full AI technology stack to all countries willing to join America's AI alliance" while preventing adversarial nations from building independent AI capabilities.

Two months later at the All-In Summit in September 2025, Tareq Amin (CEO of HUMAIN, Saudi Arabia's state-backed AI company) presented Groq as "the American AI stack in action." This was seven months after the $1.5B Saudi deal.

Sunny Madra, Groq's President and COO, was actively promoting the All-In narrative during this period. He appeared on the All-In podcast in March 2024 to provide a "Groq update" and joined Sacks on "This Week in Startups" in November 2023. When Anthropic raised AI safety regulation concerns in October 2025, Madra publicly sided with Sacks, suggesting "one company is causing chaos for the entire industry" and echoing Sacks's accusation that Anthropic was engaged in "regulatory capture."

So you have Sacks pushing an "America First" AI policy from the White House while Chamath's portfolio company (where Madra is President) is building AI infrastructure for Saudi Arabia. Then Groq gets presented at the All-In Summit as an example of American AI leadership. Three months later, Nvidia pays $20 billion to clean up the geopolitical contradiction.

## Chamath's $2 Billion Win vs. His SPAC Graveyard

After dilution from raising $1.7 billion across Series C, D, and E rounds, Social Capital's stake in Groq was probably 8-12% by the time of the Nvidia deal. At a $20 billion exit, that's $1.6 billion to $2.4 billion.

Compare that to Chamath's SPAC track record:
- IPOA (Virgin Galactic): -98.5% since inception
- IPOB (Opendoor): -62.9% (was -95% before a brief spike)
- IPOC (Clover Health): -74.4%
- IPOE (SoFi): +46% (still underperformed S&P 500's +86%)

Across 18 SPAC deals, investors averaged a 14% loss. Chamath personally dumped $213 million of Virgin Galactic stock before it crashed, using PIPE structures that let him exit while retail investors stayed locked up. In October 2025, when launching a new SPAC, he posted a warning telling retail investors not to buy it: "these vehicles are not ideal for most retail investors."

The Groq bet was classic venture capital: concentrated bet on an exceptional founder (Jonathan Ross, the engineer who invented Google's TPU) building non-obvious technology. Social Capital's 2017 internal memo projected a "High" exit scenario of $3.2 billion. They landed within range despite dilution.

But retail investors never got access to deals like Groq. They got Virgin Galactic.

## What This Actually Means

Nvidia just made its largest acquisition ever to acquire inference technology from a nine-year-old startup, structuring the deal to avoid inheriting geopolitical liabilities. That tells you several things:

**Inference is the battleground**: Training is mostly solved. Inference at scale with low latency and high efficiency is not. Nvidia paid $20 billion for deterministic inference IP because they see it as critical to the next phase of AI infrastructure.

**Geopolitics constrains deals**: The GroqCloud carve-out is precedent-setting. When startups build infrastructure for nation-states (especially ones with complicated U.S. relations), it creates acquisition complications. Expect more creative deal structures as AI infrastructure becomes geopolitically significant.

**The "America First" AI narrative is complicated**: When the people promoting American AI dominance are simultaneously cutting deals with Saudi Arabia, and those contradictions require $20 billion to resolve, it raises questions about whose interests the "American AI Alliance" actually serves.

**Access to exceptional founders is gatekept**: Chamath got access to Jonathan Ross in 2017 because of Social Capital's network and reputation. That single relationship generated a $2 billion return. Retail investors got SPACs that averaged 14% losses.

Ross said in November that Groq wasn't competing with Nvidia because "competition is a waste of money." He was right, just not in the way he meant. You can't compete with someone who has $20 billion to acquire you, especially when your own investors' geopolitical deal-making creates liabilities that only an acquisition can resolve.

The real question is what happens to GroqCloud over the next 18 months. Does it continue serving the Saudi market independently, or does it quietly wind down as Nvidia integrates the technology? My money is on the latter. The Saudi deal was valuable for forcing Nvidia's hand, but it's now a liability. Expect a graceful exit.
